# Writing Notes

- Lens 1 — Equipping AI with ToM (brief contextualisation)
  - Two distinct research directions on ToM have emerged in HAI; the first focuses on building AI systems with ToM-like capabilities — reviewed here only to contextualise the attribution direction *(Wang et al., 2024a, CHI EA)*
  - Foundational computational approach: Bayesian Theory of Mind (BToM) — models mental-state inference as inverse planning under uncertainty; shown to accurately predict human mentalising judgements *(Baker et al., 2017, Nature Human Behaviour)*
  - More recently, the question has shifted to LLMs: Kosinski *(2024, PNAS)* found GPT-4 solved 75% of false-belief tasks, raising the possibility of spontaneous ToM emergence; Chen et al. *(2024, ACL)* and Ullman *(2023, arXiv preprint)* demonstrated that performance collapses under minor task variations, suggesting use of heuristics instead of mentalising *(Aru et al., 2023)* — the question remains actively contested
- Lens 2 — Human Attribution of ToM to AI (primary focus)
  - Users routinely and spontaneously attribute mental states — intentions, knowledge, emotions — to AI systems during interaction; this attribution shapes how they engage with and rely on AI (*Wang et al., 2021, CHI)*
  - Attribution has measurable consequences: users with inaccurate mental models of AI capabilities show degraded task performance — either over-relying on or underutilising AI support *(Jussupow et al., 2021, Information Systems Research)*
  - Mental-state attributions to LLMs — including attributions of consciousness — significantly predict user trust and reliance on AI advice, independently of whether those attributions are scientifically warranted *(Colombatto et al., 2025, Communications Psychology)*
  - The contested status of genuine ToM in LLMs makes the human attribution direction independently important: regardless of what the AI actually "has," users attribute ToM and act on those attributions
- Mutual Theory of Mind (MToM) as unifying framework
  - Both directions are increasingly understood as two sides of a single paradigm — Mutual Theory of Mind (MToM) — in which both human and AI construct and revise models of each other *(Wang et al., 2024a, CHI EA; Wang & Goel, 2024, ToMinHAI@CHI)*
- Intersection with XAI
  - Explainability is established as essential for building appropriate trust and acceptance of AI systems; methods like LIME and SHAP address discriminative models, but no equivalent framework exists for generative AI *(Arrieta et al., 2020, Information Fusion; Ribeiro et al., 2016, KDD)*
  - Even for discriminative models, current explanation methods are often too technical for lay users to form accurate mental models *(Bienefeld et al., 2023, npj Digital Medicine)*
  - Explanations are the primary channel through which AI communicates its internal state and shapes users' mental models — directly linking XAI design to ToM attribution outcomes
- *Miller (2019)* notes, that ToM is required for the social process of explaining (between humans)
  - minimum is to keep track of what has been explained
  - considering knowledge & BDI to tailor explanations to user (*Halpern & Pearl (2005)*)
