# Wrtinting Notes

- much goes back on paper by *Premack & Woodruff (1976)* investigating the ToM in chimpanzees
- definition of the term as the capability to infer mental states of others
    - primary aspects are beliefs, desires & intentions (BDI) (*Wellman (2014)*) all formulated from a subjective point of view
        - **beliefs:** subjective view of the world
        - **desires:** subjective goals for what the world should look like
        - **intentions:** (future) acts to reach the desired state
    - interrelation helps to fill in gaps in the three aspects above (*Rakoczy (2022)*), e.g., if one knows of another persons beliefs and desires, they can try to rationally infer the others intentions/actions to realise their desires
- foundation is (subjective) meta-representation (*Rakoczy (2022)*)
    - knowledge and capability that others perceive the world from their point of view
    - four specific signatures (definitiely steal the table from *Rakoczy (2022)*)
        - **difference:** others perceptions can differ from one's own
        - **incompatibility:** others representation of the world can be incompatible with one's own
        - **mis-representation:** other representation/perception can be wrong/inaccurate (see also "false-belief")
        - **aspectuality:** representations of other may only consider some aspects of reality, while ignoring others
    - enables a variety of social interaction (*Rakoczy (2022)*)
        - allows for **we-perspective** (i.e., group of individuals with shared goals and intentions)
        - allows for **you-perspective**, enabling exchange and rationale discourse about perspectives
- **false-belief** plays a key role in ToM, but not the only relevant benchmark (*Wellman et al. (2001)*)
    - much researched in children by presenting small scenarios (like *Wimmer & Perner (1983)*):
        - procedure
            1. Maxi puts chocolate in a kitchen cupboard
            2. Maxi leaves the kitchen to play (such that he cannot observe the kitchen)
            3. While Maxi plays his mother moves the chocolate to a drawer
            4. **Question:** When Maxi returns, where will he look for the chocolate
        - findings
            - three year olds all (wrongly) asserted Maxi will look in the drawer
            - four- and five-year-olds (correctly) assert that Maxi will look in the cupboard about 60% of the time
            - older children (up to nine years) mostly assert correctly (86%)
    - understanding false-belief requires understanding, that people act based on their beliefs (or mental states) and that these beliefs can diverge from reality (*Wellman et al. (2001)*)
        - false-belief experiments have often been used as major determinant of young children's understanding of the mental state of others and by extension ToM (*Wellman et al. (2001)*)
        - *Wellman et al. (2001)* suggest that false-belief far from the only determinant of ToM understanding
- developed in humans during early childhood in several phases (*Wellman (2014)*)
    1. understand that people desire different things
    2. understand that people belief different things (even about the same situation)
    3. realise that people can lack access to certain knowledge
    4. Understand concept of "false-belief"
- two systems working to track ToM (*Apperly and Butterfill (2009)*)
    - two systems for belief reasoning (*Apperly and Butterfill (2009)*)
        - one efficient, but inflexible (type 1 processing)
        - one flexile, but cognitively demanding (type 2 processing)
    - aligned with limited capabilities of infants and nonumans limited capabilities in other areas (number perception and general reasoning)
    - fits in with general concepts of dual-process theory
