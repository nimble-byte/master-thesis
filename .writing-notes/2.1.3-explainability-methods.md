# Writing Notes

- explanations are important for building trust, understanding, and accountability in ML systems (*Miller (2019)*)
- different types of ML models (**black-box**, **glass-box**)
    - "black-box" terminology popularised in the mid-2010s, e.g., by *Ribeiro et al. (2016)* in research, *Castelvecchi (2016)* in science communications
    - used to describe models that are not inherently interpretable/transparent
    - contrasted with inherently transparent "glass-box" models (*Rei (2020)*)
    - alternatively called **black-box** and **white-box** models (*Molnar (2025)*)
- general modes of explanation
    - global vs. local explanations (*Rai (2020)*, *Ali et al. (2023)*)
        - local explanations focus on a single decision/output
        - global explanations offer observations about general behaviour of a model
    - **post-hoc** vs **ante-hoc**/**transparency** explanations (Arrieta et al. (2020), *Retzlaff et el. (2024)*)
        - **transparency**/**ante-hoc** models are interpretable in themselves (e.g., decision trees)
        - **post-hoc** explanations are generated through surrogate models / proxies
        - highly correlated with black-box vs. glass-box distinction, since only glass box models really possess the capability for transparency as explanation approach
        - **post-hoc** as more frequently used approach since "black-box" models tend to perform better (*Ali et al. (2023)*)
- LIME (*Ribeiro et al. (2016)*): model-agnostic, post-hoc
    - local explanations, i.e., explains a single prediction by approximating the model locally with an interpretable surrogate model (e.g., linear regression) (*Ribeiro et al. (2016)*)
    - generates perturbed samples around the instance being explained and observes how the model's predictions change (*Ribeiro et al. (2016)*)
    - benefits
        - model-agnostic
        - relatively fast to compute
    - drawbacks
        - explanations can be unstable, as they depend on the choice of perturbed samples and the surrogate model (*Lundberg & Lee (2017)*, *Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola (2018)*)
        - surrogate model approach may be misleading to users, as it may not accurately capture the behaviour of the original model (c.f. *Miller (2019)*)
- SHAP (*Lundberg & Lee (2017)*): model-agnostic, post-hoc
    - additive feature attribution, i.e. the contribution of each input feature to the output is calculated (*Lundberg & Lee (2017)*)
    - can be used for both local and global explanations (*Lundberg & Lee (2017)*)
    - based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory, which provide a "fair" distribution of a payout (the model's output) among the players (input features) (*Shapley (1953)*)
        - calculates the contribution of each feature for all possible subsets of features
        - checks how the model's output changes when a feature is included vs. excluded
        - average contribution across all subsets to determine the overall importance of each feature
    - benefits
        - model-agnostic and relatively uniform across different types of models (*Lundberg & Lee (2017)*)
        - force plots as useful visualisations for understanding feature contributions (*Martens et al. (2025)*)
    - drawbacks
        - computationally expensive, due to exponential number of feature subsets (*Lundberg et al. (2019)*)
        - assumes feature independence, which does not hold in practice (*Aas et al. (2021)*)
- Chain-of-Thought Explanations (*Wei et al. (2022)*)
    - specific to generative models, particularly large language models (LLMs)
    - prompts the model to generate a step-by-step reasoning process leading to its final answer (*Wei et al. (2022)*); can even be used "zero-shot" without examples (*Kojima et al. (2022)*)
    - benefits
        - can provide insights into the model's reasoning process and decision-making
        - can improve performance on complex tasks by encouraging the model to break down problems into smaller steps (*Wei et al. (2022)*)
    - drawbacks
        - explanations may not always be accurate or faithful to the model's actual reasoning process (*Lyu et al. (2023)*)
        - models themselves generate the explanations, exposing them to the same biases and limitations as the model's outputs (*Lyu et al. (2023)*)
