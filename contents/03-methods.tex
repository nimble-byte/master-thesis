\subsection{Research Paradigm} \label{ssec:research-paradigm}

\ac{DSR} is a research methodology that focuses on applying design as research method. On an abstract level, \ac{DSR} aims to generate new (design) knowledge through the creation of artefacts \parencite{Hevner2004,Vaishnavi2015}. Practically, the produced artefacts are often computing systems, including hard- or software \parencite[e.g.,][]{Toreini2018}. Theories, models, and design principles are often generated as complementary outcomes of \ac{DSR}, but can also be the primary outcome of a \ac{DSR} project \parencite[e.g.,][]{Bitzer2016, Scheider2023}.

Depending on the research setting, goals and questions researchers can choose from a variety of \ac{DSR} methodologies, which differ in their process and activities. A key distinction between them is the underlying research paradigm, which distinguish \textbf{positivist} and \textbf{interpretive} research methods \parencite{Venable2017}. Positivist methods are, based on the assumption, that there is an objective reality, that can be observed and measured. In contrast, interpretive methods are based on the assumption that reality is socially constructed and subjective, and that understanding it requires interpreting the meanings and experiences of individuals \parencite{Chen2004}. Consequently, \textcite{Venable2017} suggest selecting a positivist method when three conditions for a research project are met:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The research topic is governed by a general set of rules or principles.
    \item An optimal solution to the problem can be found.
    \item The researcher believes that findings from research are objective.
\end{enumerate}

If any of these conditions are not met, an interpretive method should be chosen. For this thesis, the second belief is not met, as the open-ended nature of the research questions does not allow for an optimal solution. Additionally, the field of \ac{HCI} often applies qualitative methods, due to the complexity of interactions between humans and computers \parencite{Adams2008}. Therefore, this thesis will follow an interpretive research paradigm and apply an interpretive \ac{DSR} method.

\textcite{Moeller2020} developed the \ac{MDPD} as codified steps for generating design principles. The method proposes seven steps, which can be grouped into three phases: problem explication (steps \rom{1} through \rom{3}), design principle development (steps \rom{4} through \rom{6}), and design principle evaluation (step \rom{7}). The first phase focuses on understanding the problem in its context and deciding on a research approach. Depending on the research approach, the second phase prescribes two different processes for generating design principles. With the \textbf{supportive} approach, design principles are generated based on research conducted before the creation of an artefact. In contrast, the \textbf{reflective} approach derives design principles from the analysis of an artefact. The final phase of \ac{MDPD} focuses on evaluating the generated design principles, which can be done through various methods, such as expert evaluation or empirical testing. The entire process of \ac{MDPD} is visualised in \ref{fig:mdpd}.

    \caption{Visualisation of the \ac{MDPD} \parencite{Moeller2020}.}
    \label{fig:mdpd}

While the \ac{MDPD} provides a number of steps for generating design principles, it does not prescribe specific methods for each step. Instead, it allows researchers to choose methods that are appropriate for their research context and goals \parencite{Moeller2020}. While phases \rom{1} through \rom{3} are generally applicable, the choice of methods for phases \rom{4} through \rom{7} depends on the research approach and the nature of the artefact being developed. Steps \rom{4} through \rom{6} are often realised through \ac{DSR} methods as demonstrated by \parencite{Scheider2023}. Depending on the artefact, the evaluation can be performed on a theoretical level, through expert interviews or through instantiation of the design principles in an artefact and subsequent empirical testing \parencite{Moeller2020}. Independent of the chosen evaluation method, the assessment of design principles should consider two complementary dimensions: their rigour, referring to formal qualities such as prescriptiveness and appropriate abstractedness, and their relevance, referring to their practical value, including aspects such as novelty, usefulness, accessibility, and guidance\parencite{Moeller2020}.
