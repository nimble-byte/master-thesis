\subsection{Explainable Artificial Intelligence} \label{ssec:xai}

This section provides an introduction to the field of \ac{XAI}, beginning with a definition of key terminology and general concepts related to explanations in section \ref{sssec:terminology}. Section \ref{sssec:generative-vs-discriminative} contrasts generative and discriminative models, which is a fundamental distinction relevant for the contribution of this thesis. Lastly, section \ref{sssec:xai-methods} gives an overview of commonly used \ac{XAI} methods, as well as methods that are particularly relevant for the research conducted in this thesis.

\subsubsection{Terminology} \label{sssec:terminology}

The term of \ac{XAI} has been used in the literature with varying definitions and overlapping concepts \parencite{Arrieta2020}. This thesis adopts the definition provided by \textcite{Arrieta2020}, which decomposes the definition of the term into a dictionary based definition of \enquote{explanation} and a definition for the field of \ac{AI} to arrive at a definition for an \ac{XAI} system. The Cambridge Dictionary defines an \textit{explanation} as \enquote{the details or reasons that someone gives to make something clear or easy to understand} \parencite{CambridgeDictionaryExplanation}. \enquote{\acl{AI}} refers to the field of computer science that focuses on creating systems capable of performing tasks that typically require human intelligence \parencite{Russel2020}. Combining these definitions, \textcite{Arrieta2020} defines an \ac{XAI} system as one that produces details or reasons to make its functioning understandable for a specific audience.

In literature on \ac{XAI}, the terms \enquote{explainability} and \enquote{interpretability} are often used interchangeably, although they are conceptually distinct \parencite{Arrieta2020}. \textit{Interpretability} refers to a passive quality of a machine learning model, describing the extent to which a human can assign meaning to a model, its behaviour, or its output \parencite{Arrieta2020}. \textit{Explainability}, in contrast, focuses on the active process of providing explanations that support human understanding and decision-making \parencite{Guidotti2018}. Explainability therefore represents one mode of interpretability \parencite{Lipton2018}. Lastly, \textit{transparency} represents a specific form of interpretability, describing how directly a model can be inspected without additional explanation interfaces \parencite{Lipton2018}. The relation of the terms is visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:xai-concept-relations}.

Insights from cognitive psychology further sharpen this terminology. Explanations are typically framed as answers to why-questions \parencite{Overton2011} aimed to satisfy the explainee's information need \parencite{Lipton1990}. \textcite{Miller2019} identifies three common types of explanatory questions: \textit{what}, \textit{how}, and \textit{why} questions, which are associated with different types of reasoning to produce the explanation. \textit{What}-questions are based on associative reasoning providing information about which unobserved events may have occurred given the observed outcome. \textit{How}-questions are based on interventionist reasoning, investigating if a change in the situation would alter the outcome. \textit{Why}-questions are based on counterfactual reasoning, comparing alternative causes to explain why one outcome happened instead of another. The types of questions are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:question-types-explanations} illustrating the different types of explanations produced for the same scenario, in which a student receives an incorrect recommendation from a learning-support model.

The different types of explanations are relevant for \ac{XAI}, where explanation quality is not only technical correctness, but also perceived usefulness for the target audience \parencite{Miller2019,Lombrozo2006}. The questions of \textit{what} and \textit{how} are relevant for understanding the overall model behaviour, which is of interest for experts and developers \parencite{Miller2019}. In contrast, users of \ac{XAI} systems are often more interested in answers to \textit{why} questions, that provide sufficient causes for a systems output \parencite{Kelley1987}. The rationale for this is that the computation and comprehension of full causal chains requires significant cognitive effort \parencite{Lipton1990}. The determination of what provides a sufficient cause for an outcome is influenced by the subjective perception of the explainee, which is shaped by their prior knowledge and beliefs \parencite{Kashima1998}, which will be further discussed in section \ref{sssec:theory-of-mind-xai}.

\subsubsection{Generative vs. Discriminative Models} \label{sssec:generative-vs-discriminative}

In the context of this thesis, the distinction between \textit{generative} and \textit{discriminative} models is of particular importance, as it has significant implications in model capabilities and interactions \parencite{Schneider2024}. These two classes of models differ fundamentally in their modelling objectives and outputs, which in turn shapes the design of \ac{XAI} methods and the nature of explanations required to understand them \parencite{Kumarage2026}.

\textit{Discriminative} models are trained to distinguish between two or more classes from each other. Conceptually, this class of models learns to approximate the mapping from input features to output labels \parencite{Kumarage2026}. Classic use cases are image classification \parencite[e.g.,][]{Lu2007}, anomaly \parencite[e.g.,][]{Edozie2025}, or fraud detection or sentiment analysis \parencite[e.g.,][]{Wankhade2022}. Depending on the specific application this mapping can be implemented using simple linear regression models, decision trees, but also more complex models such as support vector machines or deep neural networks \parencite{Schneider2024}. The output of \textit{discriminative} models is limited in size with at most a few hundred bits of information in an information theoretic sense \parencite{Schneider2024}. The output is also often deterministic, and all possible outputs are known at training time \parencite{Kumarage2026}.

By contrast, \textit{generative} models are trained to learn the underlying data distribution of a given dataset, and to generate new samples that are similar to the training data \parencite{Banh2023}. Applications like text generation \parencite[e.g.,][]{Brown2020} and image synthesis \parencite[e.g.,][]{Rombach2022} are common use cases for generative models. Enabled by advancements in deep learning, such as transformer-based architectures, generative models have become increasingly powerful and versatile in recent years \parencite[e.g.,][]{OpenAI2025a}. Due to their open ended-nature, the outputs of generative models are virtually unlimited in size, but often contain tens of thousands of bits of information \parencite{Schneider2024}. The outputs are also often non-deterministic \parencite{Kumarage2026}.

These differences in task and output characteristics between \textit{generative} and \textit{discriminative} models have significant implications for \ac{XAI}. Explainability approaches for discriminative models are often built on the assumption that model inputs can be mapped to outputs deterministically. This allows for the use of mathematical techniques such as feature attribution, counterfactual explanations, or perturbation-based methods \parencite{Kumarage2026}, that attempt to assign causal responsibility to in input features. Generative models break this assumption of determinism and their outputs are determined by series of stochastic sampling steps \parencite{Kumarage2026}. The resulting behaviour of generative models is therefore encoded in abstract latent representations that do not map cleanly to input features, uncoupling it from the inputs \parencite{Schneider2024}.

Practically, this means \ac{XAI} methods can be applied to \textit{generative} \ac{AI} models, but the resulting explanations are often unreliable \parencite{Kumarage2026}. This highlights the paradigm shift for explainability between \textit{generative} and \textit{discriminative} requires new methods for generating explanations. Attempts to adopt existing methods like LIME or SHAP, have been made and will be discussed in the following section alongside newer approaches to explainability in section \ref{sssec:xai-methods}. However, the fundamental differences between the two classes of models suggest that a more fruitful approach to \ac{XAI} for generative models is to develop methods that are specifically designed for their unique characteristics \parencite{Schneider2024}.

\subsubsection{Explainability Methods} \label{sssec:xai-methods}

Explainability methods are techniques designed to provide details about the functioning and reasons for the behaviour of machine learning models as defined in section \ref{sssec:terminology}. For less complex models, such as linear regression or decision trees, the model itself can be directly inspected to understand its behaviour (i.e, \textit{transparency}) \parencite{Molnar2025}. In contrast, more complex models, such as deep neural networks, are often considered \textit{black-box} models \parencite{Lipton2018}, which require explanations through surrogate models or other proxy techniques to understand \parencite{Retzlaff2024}. The distinction between \textit{transparent} and \textit{black-box} models correlates with the timing of explanation generation, as methods used to explain black-box models are often categorised as \textit{post-hoc}, as they generate explanations after the fact \parencite{Retzlaff2024}. In addition to the approach for providing reasons for a model's behaviour, explainability methods can also be categorised based on the scope of the explanation and the number of models they can be applied to \parencite{Ali2023}. Models can be explained either on a \textit{local} per-output basis, explaining a specific decision or output or on a \textit{global} level describing the overall behaviour of the model \parencite{Rai2020,Ali2023}. With regard to the number of models, \textit{model-specific} methods are designed for a specific model or model architecture, assuming certain properties of the model to generate explanations, while \textit{model-agnostic} methods can be applied to any model type without assumptions about its internal structure \parencite{Ali2023}.

The rest of this section describes three explainability methods that are particularly relevant for the research conducted in this thesis, LIME, SHAP, and \ac{CoT} explanations. LIME and SHAP are included as they are very widely used and have seen adoption for explaining generative models, with \ac{CoT} explanations included as they are a method unique to \acp{LLM} and can be implemented with minimal technical overhead.

\paragraph*{LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations)}

LIME is a model-agnostic method designed to explain individual predictions (i.e., explain locally) \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. The core mechanism of Lime is a local approximation of the target model's behaviour around the output instance being explained. Explanations are generated by creating a set of perturbed samples around the instance of interest (e.g., by changing or removing features) and observing the changes in the target model's predictions \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. An interpretable surrogate model, such as linear regression, is then fitted to this perturbed data. The surrogate model provides a local explanation by highlighting which input features could have influenced the specific prediction. An example of LIME's perturbation and surrogate model fitting process is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:lime-perturbations}.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{images/fig-lime.png}
    \caption[Example of LIME]{Example of LIME fitting a linear surrogate model (dotted line) for a specific instance (red cross). The perturbed samples (light red cross and blue dots) are scaled by their proximity to the instance being explained. \textit{Reused from \textcite{Ribeiro2016}}.}
    \label{fig:lime-perturbations}
\end{figure}

LIME's free choice of surrogate model and perturbation strategy allows it to be applied to virtually any model \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. The computational requirements of LIME are also relatively low, as it only requires a limited number of perturbations and a simple surrogate model to generate explanations \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. However, the quality of LIME explanations is sensitive to the choice of perturbation strategy \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. Combined with the local nature of the explanations, this can lead to instability in the explanations generated \parencite{Alvarez2018}. Additionally, the surrogate model may not correctly capture the behaviour of the original model, leading to explanations that are not faithful to the model's actual decision-making process \parencite{Miller2019}. An application of LIME to generative models is challenging, as the simplicity of surrogate models does not allow capturing high-level concepts \parencite{Kumarage2026}. Despite this, the general concept of LIME has seen adoption in larger frameworks for \ac{GenAI} explanation \parencite[e.g.,][]{Ahmed2025, Durango2025}.

\paragraph*{SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)}

SHAP is a model-agnostic method that functions through additive feature attributions \parencite{Lundberg2017}. In principle, SHAP calculates how important each input feature is for the model's output. For this purpose the method uses Shapley values from cooperative game theory, which provide a way to \enquote{fairly} distribute a payout (i.e., model output) among players based on their contribution (importance) to the total payout \parencite{Shapley1953}. To accomplish this, SHAP evaluates the contribution of each feature across all possible subsets of features, checking how the model's output changes when a feature is included versus excluded, and averages these contributions to determine overall feature importance \parencite{Lundberg2017,Shapley1953}. The method can be used to produce local or global explanations, depending on whether the feature attributions are calculated for a specific instance or averaged across multiple instances \parencite{Lundberg2017}. Local explanations can be visualised using force plots, such as the example in Figure~\ref{fig:shap-force-plot}, showing a local explanation for the decision to reject a loan application.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/fig-shap_force_plot.jpg}
    \caption[SHAP force plot]{A force plot visualising the local feature attributions calculated by SHAP for a rejected loan application. Features are split into positively contributing features (in red on the left) and negatively contributing features (in blue on the right). The size of the bars indicates the magnitude of the contribution. \textit{Reused from \textcite{Martens2025}}.}
    \label{fig:shap-force-plot}
\end{figure}

SHAP's robust theoretical foundation provides a strong basis for its explanations, yielding consistent and theoretically sound feature attributions \parencite{Alvarez2018}. The utilisation of force plots allows for intuitive visualisations, that can be understood by non-experts \parencite{Martens2025}. However, the computational cost of SHAP scales exponentially with the number of features, making it impractical for models with many input features, like \acp{LLM} \parencite{Lundberg2017}. Additionally, SHAP's assumption of feature independence can lead to incorrect explanations in cases where features are correlated, which is often the case in real-world data \parencite{Aas2021}. Similar to LIME, the application of SHAP to generative models is challenging, as the method's reliance on feature attributions does not align well with the abstract latent representations used by generative models \parencite{Kumarage2026}. Still SHAP has seen adoption in combination partial dependence plots to explain generative models through proxies \parencite{Wang2023}.

\paragraph*{Chain-of-Thought Explanations}

\ac{CoT} is an approach to interpretability that is specific to \acp{LLM}. The foundational idea is to have the model follow a step-by-step reasoning process leading to the final answer \parencite{Wei2022}. These intermediate steps can then be used as explanations for the model's final answer, in the sense of the explanation provided in Section~\ref{sssec:terminology}, as they provide details about the model's reasoning process. The step-by-step reasoning can be elicited through training \parencite{Kim2023} or even zero-shot prompting \parencite{Kojima2022}, making it a method that can be implemented with minimal technical overhead. A central criticism of \ac{CoT} explanations is that they may not faithfully reflect the model's actual internal reasoning process \parencite{Lyu2023}. Since the model generates its own explanations, these explanations inherit the same biases and limitations as the model's outputs \parencite{Lyu2023}. Despite these limitations, \ac{CoT} explanations have shown promise in improving performance on complex tasks by encouraging systematic problem decomposition \parencite{Wei2022}, and they provide a unique avenue for gaining insight into the reasoning processes of \acp{LLM}.

\subsubsection{Relevant Concepts from Cognitive Psychology} \label{sssec:cognitive-psychology-concepts}

Before discussing \ac{ToM} it is important to introduce two general concepts from cognitive psychology that are relevant for understanding the role of \ac{ToM} in \ac{XAI}. These concepts are \textit{dual-process theory} and \textit{heuristics}. Both concepts are important for understanding how users interact with \ac{AI} systems and how they reflect on these interactions \parencite{Jussupow2021}. They are also important for understanding the cognitive processes involved with evaluating explanations provided by \ac{XAI} systems \parencite{Miller2019}.

\textit{Dual-process theory} is a group of theories that propose the existence of two distinct types of cognitive processing in humans \parencite{Evans2013}, generically labelled as \textit{Type 1} and \textit{Type 2} processing. Different theories ascribe different characteristics to these two types of processing, but they generally agree on the distinction between an intuitive, automatic \textit{Type 1} processing and a more deliberate, controlled \textit{Type 2} processing \parencite{Kahnemann2011}. Beyond these common characteristics, different theories ascribe different additional characteristics to the two types of processing, such as speed, parallelism, consciousness, and response bias \parencite{Evans2013}. Table~\ref{tab:type1-type2-processing} summarises the common characteristics alongside a selection of varying characteristics that are supported by several theories but not universally agreed upon.

Within dual-process theory, different relationships between the two types of processing are proposed. Default-interventionist accounts propose that \textit{Type 1} processing generates an initial response that \textit{Type 2} may subsequently override \parencite{Evans2013}. Parallel-competitive accounts, on the other hand, assume that both processes can operate in parallel and that conflicts are resolved between them if they arise \parencite{Evans2013}. For the purposes of this thesis, both accounts are acknowledged, but the default-interventionist perspective is used as the primary working assumption, as empirical evidence is more supportive of this account \parencite{Evans2013, Kahnemann2011}. Additionally, with the parallel-competitive account would require the fast \textit{Type 1} to wait for the slower \textit{Type 2} processing to resolve conflicts, which would eliminate the speed advantage of \textit{Type 1} processing \parencite{Evans2013}.

\textit{Heuristics} are methods for problem-solving that simplify the problem, to find a solution faster in favour of a non-optimised solution. The core mechanic of these mental shortcuts is to ignore part of the information available for finding an optimal solution \parencite{Gigerenzer2011}. The research on heuristics goes back on the theory of bounded rationality stating that human decision-making is characterised by limited time, mental resources and information \parencite{Simon1956}. Despite these limitations, humans were able to make decisions that yielded satisfactory results, leading \textcite{Simon1956} to coin the term \enquote{satisficing}; a portmanteau of \enquote{satisfy} and \enquote{suffice}. It describes the behavioural pattern of choosing the first acceptable solution instead of searching for an optimal solution \parencite{Hjeij2023}. Heuristics also play an important role in dual-process theory, as they are often associated with \textit{Type 1} processing \parencite{PLACEHOLDER}. In computer science and \ac{IS} research heuristics find frequent application to solve problems, that would otherwise be too complex to solve \parencite{Hjeij2023}. A classic example for this is the travelling salesman problem, to which a good solution can be found quite efficiently, but devising the optimal solution might not even be possible.

\subsubsection{Theory of Mind} \label{sssec:theory-of-mind}

\ac{ToM} refers to the cognitive capability to attribute mental states to oneself and others and to understand that others have beliefs, desires, and intentions that may differ from one's own \parencite{Wellman2014}. The foundational work on \ac{ToM} originates from research by \textcite{Premack1978}, who investigated whether chimpanzees possess the ability to infer mental states of others. Today's research on \ac{ToM} in humans investigates a large variety of phenomena, such as the development of \ac{ToM} in children \parencite{Wellman2014}, factors affecting \ac{ToM} performance in adults \parencite{Apperly2009}, and the role of \ac{ToM} in psychological disorders such as autism spectrum disorder \parencite{BaronCohen1995}.

The core of \ac{ToM} is the understanding that individuals hold subjective mental representations of the world and act according to these representations. Literature on \ac{ToM} often focuses on three primary components that form the basis of \ac{ToM}: \textit{beliefs}, \textit{desires}, and \textit{intentions} (BDI) \parencite{Wellman2014}. \textit{Beliefs} represent fundamental facts about the world as perceived by an individual. \textit{Desires} reflect subjective goals for what an individual wants the world to look like. \textit{Intentions} represent planned future actions aimed at satisfying \textit{desires} given the individual's \textit{beliefs}. These components are deeply interrelated and form the basis for \textit{belief reasoning} (or \textit{belief-desire reasoning}), which is the ability to make rational inferences about others' actions based on their \textit{beliefs} and \textit{desires} \parencite{Wellman2014, Rakoczy2022}. For instance, knowing another person's \textit{beliefs} and \textit{desires} allows one to infer their likely \textit{intentions} or actions to satisfy those \textit{desires}. A complete example for BDI is shown in Table~\ref{tab:bdi-example}. Some researchers have proposed additional components that act as moderators of the BDI components, such as \textit{emotions} and \textit{thoughts} \parencite{Apperly2009}. To keep the discussion concise, this thesis focuses on the BDI components as the core of \ac{ToM}, while acknowledging that other components may also play a role in \ac{ToM} reasoning.

The general capability enabling \ac{ToM} is called \textit{meta-representation}, which is the understanding that others perceive and represent the world from their own subjective point of view \parencite{Perner1991}. This meta-representational understanding of others is characterised by four distinct signatures, which are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:tom-signatures}. \textit{Difference} refers to the understanding that others' perceptions may differ from one's own opening the possibility for different perspectives. \textit{Incompatibility} describes the notion that these different perspectives may be fundamentally incompatible with each other. \textit{Misrepresentation} captures the understanding that others' representations may be inaccurate or incorrect relative to reality. Finally, \textit{aspectuality} reflects the understanding that others' representations may only consider certain aspects of reality while ignoring others \parencite{Rakoczy2022}.

The signature of \textit{misrepresentation} is of particular interest for \ac{ToM} research, as it is fundamental to the notion of \textit{false-beliefs}. The ability to understand that others can hold beliefs that are false relative to reality has long been considered a key benchmark for \ac{ToM} competence of children \parencite{Wellman2001}. The understanding of \textit{false-beliefs} is most often tested using scenarios similar to the Wimmer-Perner task \parencite{Wimmer1983}, which is a four-step scenario involving a character (Maxi):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Maxi places chocolate in a cupboard and leaves the room.
    \item While Maxi is absent, the chocolate is moved to a drawer.
    \item Maxi returns to the room.
    \item The child is asked where Maxi will look for the chocolate.
\end{enumerate}

When asked where Maxi will look for the chocolate upon returning, three-year-olds typically answer incorrectly (the drawer), while four- and five-year-olds answer correctly approximately 60\% of the time, with accuracy improving to 86\% in older children \parencite{Wimmer1983}. Despite this, \textcite{Wellman2001} argues that false-belief is a benchmark to be used cautiously for determining \ac{ToM} competence, especially in young children: other factors such as experimental setting and individual differences of participants can influence the performance on \textit{false-belief} tasks. Nonetheless, the understanding of the concept \textit{false-belief} represents a significant milestone in the development of \ac{ToM} in children, as it demonstrates the ability to understand that others can hold beliefs that are not only different from one's own but also incorrect relative to reality \parencite{Wellman2014}.

\textcite{Apperly2009} propose a \enquote{two-systems} account of belief reasoning, which posits the existence of two distinct cognitive systems for belief reasoning. The first system is efficient but inflexible, while the second system is flexible but cognitively demanding. They draw parallels to other areas of cognition like number perception, where adults are capable of quickly comparing two numbers or group sizes with one another without making a conscious effort \parencite{Apperly2009}. This shows a parallel to dual-process theories of cognition, where the first system is associated with Type 1 processing and the second system is associated with Type 2 processing.
