\subsection{Explainable Artificial Intelligence} \label{ssec:xai}

This section provides an introduction to the field of \ac{XAI}, beginning with a definition of key terminology and general concepts related to explanations in section \ref{sssec:terminology}. Section \ref{sssec:generative-vs-discriminative} contrasts generative and discriminative models, which is a fundamental distinction relevant for the contribution of this thesis. Lastly, section \ref{sssec:xai-methods} gives an overview of commonly used \ac{XAI} methods, as well as methods that are particularly relevant for the research conducted in this thesis.

\subsubsection{Terminology} \label{sssec:terminology}

The term of \ac{XAI} has been used in the literature with varying definitions and overlapping concepts \parencite{Arrieta2020}. This thesis adopts the definition provided by \textcite{Arrieta2020}, which decomposes the definition of the term into a dictionary based definition of \enquote{explanation} and a definition for the field of \ac{AI} to arrive at a definition for an \ac{XAI} system. The Cambridge Dictionary defines an \textit{explanation} as \enquote{the details or reasons that someone gives to make something clear or easy to understand} \parencite{CambridgeDictionaryExplanation}. \enquote{\acl{AI}} refers to the field of computer science that focuses on creating systems capable of performing tasks that typically require human intelligence \parencite{PLACEHOLDER}. Combining these definitions, \textcite{Arrieta2020} defines an \ac{XAI} system as one that produces details or reasons to make its functioning understandable for a specific audience.

In literature on \ac{XAI}, the terms \enquote{explainability} and \enquote{interpretability} are often used interchangeably, although they are conceptually distinct \parencite{Arrieta2020}. \textit{Interpretability} refers to a passive quality of a machine learning model, describing the extent to which a human can assign meaning to a model, its behaviour, or its output \parencite{Arrieta2020}. \textit{Explainability}, in contrast, focuses on the active process of providing explanations that support human understanding and decision-making \parencite{Guidotti2018}. Explainability therefore represents one mode of interpretability \parencite{Lipton2018}. Lastly, \textit{transparency} represents a specific form of interpretability, describing how directly a model can be inspected without additional explanation interfaces \parencite{Lipton2018}. Transparency is used as contrast to black-box models that require additional explanation methods to be interpretable \parencite{Lipton2018}. Insights from cognitive psychology further sharpen this terminology. Explanations are typically framed as answers to why-questions \parencite{Overton2011} aimed to satisfy the explainee's information need \parencite{Lipton1990}. \textcite{Miller2019} identifies three common types of explanatory questions: \textit{what}, \textit{how}, and \textit{why} questions, which are associated with different types of reasoning to produce the explanation. \textit{What}-questions are based on associative reasoning providing information about which unobserved events may have occurred given the observed outcome. \textit{How}-questions are based on interventionist reasoning, investigating if a change in the situation would alter the outcome. \textit{Why}-questions are based on counterfactual reasoning, comparing alternative causes to explain why one outcome happened instead of another. The types of questions are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:question-types-explanations} illustrating the different types of explanations produced for the same scenario, in which a student receives an incorrect recommendation from a learning-support model.

The different types of explanations are relevant for \ac{XAI}, where explanation quality is not only technical correctness, but also perceived usefulness for the target audience \parencite{Miller2019,Lombrozo2006}. The questions of \textit{what} and \textit{how} are relevant for understanding the overall model behaviour, which is of interest for experts and developers \parencite{Miller2019}. In contrast, users of \ac{XAI} systems are often more interested in answers to \textit{why} questions, that provide sufficient causes for a systems output \parencite{Kelley1987}. The rationale for this is that the computation and comprehension of full causal chains requires significant cognitive effort \parencite{Lipton1990}. The determination of what provides a sufficient cause for an outcome is influenced by the subjective perception of the explainee, which is shaped by their prior knowledge and beliefs \parencite{Kashima1998}, which will be further discussed in section \ref{sssec:theory-of-mind-xai}.

\subsubsection{Generative vs. Discriminative Models} \label{sssec:generative-vs-discriminative}

In the context of this thesis, the distinction between \textit{generative} and \textit{discriminative} models is of particular importance, as it has significant implications in model capabilities and interactions \parencite{Schneider2024}. These two classes of models differ fundamentally in their modelling objectives and outputs, which in turn shapes the design of \ac{XAI} methods and the nature of explanations required to understand them \parencite{Kumarage2026}.

\textit{Discriminative} models are trained to distinguish between two or more classes from each other. Conceptually, this class of models learns to approximate the mapping from input features to output labels \parencite{Kumarage2026}. Classic use cases are image classification, anomaly, or fraud detection or sentiment analysis \parencite{PLACEHOLDER}. Depending on the specific application this mapping can be implemented using simple linear regression models, decision trees, but also more complex models such as support vector machines or deep neural networks \parencite{PLACEHOLDER}. The output of \textit{discriminative} models is limited in size with at most a few hundred bits of information in an information theoretic sense \parencite{Schneider2024}. The output is also often deterministic, and all possible outputs are known at training time \parencite{Kumarage2026}.

By contrast, \textit{generative} models are trained to learn the underlying data distribution of a given dataset, and to generate new samples that are similar to the training data \parencite{Banh2023}. Applications like text generation \parencite[e.g.,][]{Brown2020} and image synthesis \parencite[e.g.,][]{Rombach2022} are common use cases for generative models. Enabled by advancements in deep learning, such as transformer-based architectures, generative models have become increasingly powerful and versatile in recent years \parencite[e.g.,][]{OpenAI2025a}. Due to their open ended-nature, the outputs of generative models are virtually unlimited in size, but often contain tens of thousands of bits of information \parencite{Schneider2024}. The outputs are also often non-deterministic \parencite{Kumarage2026}.

These differences in task and output characteristics between \textit{generative} and \textit{discriminative} models have significant implications for \ac{XAI}. Explainability approaches for discriminative models are often built on the assumption that model inputs can be mapped to outputs deterministically. This allows for the use of mathematical techniques such as feature attribution, counterfactual explanations, or perturbation-based methods \parencite{Kumarage2026}, that attempt to assign causal responsibility to in input features. Generative models break this assumption of determinism and their outputs are determined by series of stochastic sampling steps \parencite{Kumarage2026}. The resulting behaviour of generative models is therefore encoded in abstract latent representations that do not map cleanly to input features, uncoupling it from the inputs \parencite{Schneider2024}.

Practically, this means \ac{XAI} methods can be applied to \textit{generative} \ac{AI} models, but the resulting explanations are often unreliable \parencite{Kumarage2026}. This highlights the paradigm shift for explainability between \textit{generative} and \textit{discriminative} requires new methods for generating explanations. Attempts to adopt existing methods like LIME or SHAP, have been made and will be discussed in the following section alongside newer approaches to explainability in section \ref{sssec:xai-methods}. However, the fundamental differences between the two classes of models suggest that a more fruitful approach to \ac{XAI} for generative models is to develop methods that are specifically designed for their unique characteristics \parencite{Schneider2024}.

\subsubsection{Explainability Methods} \label{sssec:xai-methods}

Explanations play a critical role in building trust, understanding, and accountability in machine learning systems \parencite{Miller2019}. The choice of explanation method depends significantly on the nature of the model being explained. In the literature, a fundamental distinction is made between black-box and glass-box models. The term \enquote{black-box} was popularised in the mid-2010s to describe models that are not inherently interpretable or transparent, appearing first in academic research \parencite{Ribeiro2016} and subsequently in science communication \parencite{Castelvecchi2016}. Black-box models are contrasted with inherently transparent glass-box models \parencite{Rai2020}, which are alternatively termed white-box models \parencite{Molnar2025}.

Explainability methods can be categorised along two primary dimensions: the scope of the explanation and the timing of explanation generation. With regard to scope, explanations are either global or local \parencite{Rai2020,Ali2023}. Local explanations focus on a single decision or output, providing insight into why a specific prediction was made. Global explanations, by contrast, offer observations about the general behaviour and patterns learned by the model. Regarding timing, methods are distinguished as post-hoc or ante-hoc (also called transparency-based) \parencite{Arrieta2020,Retzlaff2024}. Ante-hoc or transparency-based methods produce interpretable models where the decision-making process is inherently understandable, such as decision trees. Post-hoc methods generate explanations through surrogate models or other proxy techniques after the fact, and are the more frequently used approach because black-box models often achieve superior performance \parencite{Ali2023}. This distinction is highly correlated with the black-box versus glass-box distinction, as only glass-box models truly possess the capacity for transparency as an explanation approach.

\paragraph*{LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations)}

LIME is a model-agnostic, post-hoc method designed to explain individual predictions \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. It works by approximating the behaviour of the target model locally with an interpretable surrogate model, such as linear regression. To generate an explanation, LIME creates a series of perturbed samples around the instance being explained and observes how the target model's predictions change in response to these perturbations \parencite{Ribeiro2016}. By fitting the surrogate model to this perturbed data, LIME produces a local linear approximation that highlights which input features were influential for the specific prediction. A key strength of LIME is its model-agnostic nature, making it applicable to any model type, and its computational efficiency. However, LIME explanations can be unstable, as they depend on the selection of perturbed samples and the choice of surrogate model \parencite{Lundberg2017,Alvarez2018}. Additionally, the surrogate model approach may produce misleading explanations if it does not accurately capture the behaviour of the original model, potentially giving users false confidence in their understanding of the model \parencite{Miller2019}.

\paragraph*{SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)}

SHAP provides additive feature attribution by calculating the contribution of each input feature to the model's output \parencite{Lundberg2017}. Unlike LIME, SHAP can generate both local and global explanations. The method is grounded in cooperative game theory, specifically in Shapley values, which provide a principled way to fairly distribute a payout among players \parencite{Shapley1953}. In the context of SHAP, the payout is the model's output, and the players are the input features. SHAP calculates the contribution of each feature across all possible subsets of features, checks how the model's output changes when a feature is included versus excluded, and averages these contributions to determine overall feature importance. This theoretical foundation gives SHAP significant advantages in terms of consistency across different model types \parencite{Lundberg2017}. Force plots provide effective visualisations for understanding individual feature contributions and their cumulative effect on the prediction \parencite{Martens2025}. The primary limitation of SHAP is its computational expense, arising from the exponential number of feature subsets that must be evaluated \parencite{Lundberg2017}. Additionally, SHAP assumes feature independence, which often does not hold in practice, potentially leading to unrealistic explanations \parencite{Aas2021}.

\paragraph*{Chain-of-Thought Explanations}

Chain-of-Thought explanations represent a distinct approach to explainability, specific to generative models and particularly large language models. Rather than decomposing model internals, this method prompts the model to generate step-by-step reasoning leading to its final answer \parencite{Wei2022}. Remarkably, chain-of-thought prompting can be effective even in zero-shot settings without explicit examples \parencite{Kojima2022}. The appeal of this approach lies in the potential to gain insight into the model's reasoning process and in its ability to improve performance on complex tasks by encouraging systematic problem decomposition \parencite{Wei2022}. However, significant limitations exist: the explanations generated by the model may not faithfully reflect the model's actual internal reasoning process \parencite{Lyu2023}, and since the model generates its own explanations, these explanations inherit the same biases and limitations as the model's outputs \parencite{Lyu2023}.
